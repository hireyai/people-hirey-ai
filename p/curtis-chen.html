Transcript

Today we met Curtis Chen, an international student from Taiwan who recently graduated from UCSD. He studied cognitive science with machine learning and neurocomputation, a path that combines computer science, data science, neuroscience, psychology, and the study of how people think. Curtis explains Startup Incubator, the UCSD student startup community he helped build: students often have strong ideas but need help forming a team, learning startup culture and business basics, finding resources, and recruiting collaborators. The goal is to help them move from an idea toward a real company. Curtis says cognitive science matters in this AI era because technology still needs to become more human. He started coding later than many people and realized that instead of competing only on raw coding speed, he could combine technical skills with an interest in psychology and human behavior. He joined Startup Incubator early through a close friend, helped the small team grow, and worked on events and campus resources for student builders. The story becomes less about one club and more about how a person changes: Curtis says that if he kept doing the same things, he would keep repeating himself, so he deliberately stepped outside his comfort zone. His startup lesson is simple: ideas are important, but execution matters more; you do not have to be the best, but you want to be fast enough to deliver.
